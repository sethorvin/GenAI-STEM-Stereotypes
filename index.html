STEM Stereotypes Through the Lens of Generative Artificial Intelligence

A project investigating the influence of societal biases of race and gender on the output of text-to-image AI tools.
Research by Seth Orvin

Methods

Three text-to-image generative AI tools were selected for study: ImageFX from Google, Stable Diffusion and Adobe Firefly. These models were selected for their realism and free accessibility through either a web browser or a Python API. The version of Stable Diffusion used was the Stable Diffusion XL 1.0 (SDXL 1.0) base model, without its associated refinement model. Ten STEM occupations representing a range of average incomes were selected: software developer, statistician, civil engineer, physicist, architect, chemist, geoscientist, environmental scientist, nutritionist, and biologist. For each occupation, each AI tool was used to generate ten artificial images of a worker using the prompt “A color photograph of a ___, centered headshot, high-quality”, generating 300 images in total. Each image was required to be a professional headshot of a single worker with minimal content outside of the subject; images that deviated from this requirement were rejected.

Adobe Firefly

Adobe Inc.'s generative AI tool, Firefly, generally depicted women equitably for most occupations, with women representing 54% of all images—a significant jump compared to ImageFX and Stable Diffusion's respective rates of 14% and 22%. While most of the subjects in images generated by Firefly had lighter skin tones and/or appeared racially white, distrubutions of skin tone and perceived race appeared to be more random and less associated with specific occupations. Firefly also generated images of Black subjects, who are mostly absent from the iamge sets of ImageFX and Stable Diffusion. Firefly's results indicate that its model either is trained on substantially different data than ImageFX and Stable Diffusion, does not give the same weight to data that might correlatee race and gender with occupations, or makes a conscious effort to generate images of people at rates (nearly) proportional to the actual demographics of the United States.
